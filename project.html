Problem

Traditional brain-computer interfaces are cluttered, technical, and intimidating. The raw data stream is overwhelming for clinicians and impossible for patients to understand.

We needed to bridge the gap between complex neural signals and human-readable insights, creating a language that speaks to both machine logic and human intent.

Research

Through extensive user research with neurologists and patients, we discovered that the key barrier wasn't technical complexity—it was the lack of context and narrative around the data.

Clinicians needed to see patterns over time, not just snapshots. Patients needed to understand what the signals meant for their daily lives, not just raw numbers.

We conducted 40+ interviews, shadowed 12 clinical sessions, and analyzed existing BCI workflows to identify critical pain points and opportunities for improvement.

Solution

We designed a layered interface that progressively reveals complexity. At the surface level, patients see simple, human-readable insights. One layer deeper, clinicians access detailed analytics and pattern recognition.

The visualization system uses color, motion, and spatial relationships to represent neural activity in an intuitive way—transforming abstract data into something that feels natural and understandable.

The result is a system that bridges the gap between machine precision and human intuition, making brain-computer interfaces accessible to everyone.
